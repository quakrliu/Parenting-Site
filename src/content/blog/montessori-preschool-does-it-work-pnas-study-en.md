# Does Montessori Actually Work? A Landmark Study Just Answered That.

*Last updated: April 2026 | Based on: PNAS, 2025*

## The Largest Study Ever Done on Public Montessori

In late 2025, researchers from the University of Virginia, the University of Pennsylvania, and the American Institutes for Research published a landmark study in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* — the first-ever **national randomized controlled trial** of public Montessori preschool.

The "randomized controlled trial" part matters enormously. This isn't a survey of Montessori parents who love the method (of course they'd be biased). This was a lottery. Families who applied to public Montessori programs were randomly accepted or waitlisted. The two groups started equal. Then researchers tracked what happened.

**The results, by the end of kindergarten:**

- **Reading proficiency:** Significantly higher in Montessori children
- **Executive function:** Stronger planning, impulse control, and self-regulation
- **Short-term memory:** Measurably better
- **Social understanding:** Higher perspective-taking and empathy
- **Math:** Positive trends across most analyses

The study followed 588 children across 24 public Montessori programs in nine states. Lead researcher Angeline Lillard of UVA summarized the findings with rare academic bluntness: *"No matter how we did it, we got a consistent story."*

David Loeb, the quantitative lead from the University of Pennsylvania, was equally direct: *"Because of the rigor of the study, they're definitely the strongest findings to date on Montessori effectiveness."*

## Three Things That Surprised Even the Researchers

### 1. This Is Public Montessori — Not the Private School You're Imagining

When most people hear "Montessori," they picture expensive private schools with $20,000-a-year tuition, organic snacks, and parents who own tasteful linen tote bags.

This entire study was on **public Montessori programs**. Lottery access. No tuition. Available to any family in those nine states.

### 2. It Costs Less, Not More

Here's where my engineering brain broke.

*"Over three years, every child in Montessori versus conventional preschool costs their districts, on average, $13,127 less,"* said Lillard.

The reason is structural: Montessori's mixed-age classrooms (ages 3-6 together) reduce the need for adult intervention because older children naturally mentor younger ones. Less overhead. More intrinsic engagement. Better outcomes at lower cost.

That combination — better results AND lower cost — almost never happens in education research. When it does, you pay attention.

### 3. The Gains Don't Fade — They Grow

Most early childhood research follows a dispiriting pattern: programs show benefits in preschool, and those benefits evaporate by 2nd or 3rd grade. It's one of the most frustrating findings in education science.

The PNAS study found the opposite. Montessori gains persisted and appeared to strengthen over time.

The reason, the researchers suggest, is what Montessori actually develops: **executive function** — the mental skills that allow children to focus, plan, regulate emotions, and persist through difficulty. Executive function isn't a subject you learn. It's the cognitive infrastructure that makes learning easier. Once built, it compounds.

## Why the 3-to-6 Window Is Not Optional

This is where I'd usually say "don't panic." But I think it's worth being honest about what the developmental science says.

Harvard's Center on the Developing Child calls the early childhood years a "sensitive period" for executive function development — a window when neural connections form at peak speed. The architecture built during ages 3-6 shapes how easily a child learns, regulates emotions, and handles frustration for years afterward.

Research consistently shows that children who develop strong executive function skills by kindergarten entry perform better not just academically, but socially — and that advantage compounds through middle school. The reverse is also true: children who enter kindergarten with weak executive function face a growing gap that becomes harder to close each year.

This isn't about putting pressure on your preschooler. It's about understanding that *how* they spend these years matters, not just that they're somewhere being kept safe.

The question isn't "Montessori or not." The question is: does your child's environment give them long stretches of self-directed, purposeful, challenging work?

## What This Means for Your Family

Let me be precise about what this study proves and what it doesn't.

It does **not** prove that every Montessori school is excellent. Quality varies enormously. A poorly implemented Montessori classroom with an undertrained teacher produces worse outcomes than a great conventional one. The study found strong results because these were real public programs with genuine Montessori implementation.

It does **not** prove that private Montessori schools — at $15,000-$30,000 per year — are worth that premium over well-run public options. This study says nothing about that question.

What it **does** prove: the core Montessori approach — child-led work periods, mixed-age learning, freedom within structure, real tasks with real consequences — produces measurable cognitive and social advantages. And notably, effects were strongest among children from **lower-income families**, making this an equity finding as much as a parenting one.

## Your Preschool Checklist (Montessori or Not)

Whether you're evaluating a Montessori program or a conventional preschool, the research now gives us a clearer framework for what to look for:

**Green flags:**
- ✅ Uninterrupted work periods of at least 90 minutes (2 hours is better)
- ✅ Children choose their own activities from a prepared set of options
- ✅ Mixed ages or at least mixed ability groupings
- ✅ Real materials for real tasks (child-sized tools, actual soil, actual food preparation)
- ✅ Teachers observe and guide rather than direct and entertain

**Red flags:**
- ❌ Constant teacher-led whole-group instruction for 3-year-olds
- ❌ Heavy screen time as an engagement tool
- ❌ Environments optimized for adult aesthetics over child access
- ❌ No opportunity for deep focus or extended project work

For Montessori specifically: look for AMI or AMS-credentialed teachers and ask how long uninterrupted work periods run. Those two things tell you most of what you need to know.

## How to Bring Montessori Home This Week

You don't need a Montessori school to use Montessori principles. Here are four that the research directly supports:

**1. Protect unstructured time.** Your child's brain develops executive function when they choose, initiate, and follow through on their own activities. Resist the urge to fill every moment. When they say "I'm bored," try: "I wonder what you could find to do?"

**2. Offer real work.** Montessori classrooms have child-sized brooms, real knives (yes, supervised), plants to water, and food to prepare. The competence and pride from real tasks — tasks with actual stakes — builds something that pretend play cannot fully replicate.

**3. Follow the obsession.** If your 4-year-old wants to spend three weeks learning everything about diggers, let them. That sustained, self-directed focus is executive function being trained. Interrupting it for "variety" is counterproductive.

**4. Fewer, better materials.** A Montessori environment has fewer toys, each chosen with purpose. [Lovevery's play kits for 4-year-olds](https://lovevery.com) are designed on exactly this principle: developmentally staged, open-ended materials that invite deep engagement without overwhelming.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Does Montessori actually work, according to research? {#faq-does-montessori-work}

Yes. The 2025 PNAS national randomized controlled trial found public Montessori preschool significantly improved reading, executive function, short-term memory, and social understanding in 588 children by the end of kindergarten — with gains that persisted and grew rather than faded over time.

### What did the 2025 PNAS Montessori study find? {#faq-pnas-study-findings}

The first national RCT of public Montessori preschool, tracking 588 children across 24 programs in nine states, found Montessori-educated children outperformed peers in reading, executive function, short-term memory, and social understanding by kindergarten's end, at an average cost savings of $13,127 per child over three years.

### Is public Montessori as effective as private Montessori preschool? {#faq-public-vs-private}

The landmark PNAS study examined public Montessori programs with trained teachers and found strong outcomes. Program quality and teacher training matter more than public vs. private status. Well-implemented public Montessori programs produced the significant gains this research documented.

### What is executive function and why does Montessori develop it? {#faq-executive-function}

Executive function includes planning, impulse control, sustained focus, and emotional self-regulation — the cognitive skills that make academic and social learning possible. Montessori develops executive function through extended self-directed work periods that require children to initiate, plan, and complete meaningful tasks independently.

### How much does public Montessori preschool cost? {#faq-cost}

Public Montessori programs are free, funded by school districts. The PNAS research found public Montessori programs cost districts $13,127 less per child over three years than conventional preschool — primarily due to the efficiency of mixed-age classrooms where older children mentor younger peers.

### At what age should a child start Montessori? {#faq-age-to-start}

Most Montessori programs begin at age 3, which aligns with the early childhood sensitive period for executive function development identified by Harvard's Center on the Developing Child. The PNAS study followed children beginning at age 3 through kindergarten completion at age 6.

### Can Montessori principles be used at home without a Montessori school? {#faq-montessori-at-home}

Yes. Core Montessori principles — protecting unstructured time, offering real meaningful work, following the child's deep interests, and providing fewer purposeful materials — can be applied at home. These practices support the same executive function development the PNAS research validated in classroom settings.
